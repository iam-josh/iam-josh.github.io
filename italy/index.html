Florence (Firenze)

Florence is the capital of the stunning Tuscany region in the centre of Italy. Surrounded by rolling hills and dotted with elegant architecture, palaces, museums and art galleries, Florence is perceived as one of the most beautiful cities in the world. Over the years, it has been home to various famous names, including Renaissance artists Michelangelo and Leonardo da Vinci, as well as the astronomer Galileo.

Travel tips

Go on a day trip to Pisa, it’s just over an hour away from Florence from train!

If you’d like to go inside the cathedral and/or climb the dome or bell tower, then it’s worth booking a slot ahead of time to avoid long queues.
https://duomo.firenze.it/en/home

Things to see

Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore : The ‘Duomo’ is an enormous and magnificent cathedral in the centre of Florence (the 3rd largest in the world). Building started in the late 13th century and took over 140 years to complete! At one end is the striking Cupola di Brunelleschi, the largest brick dome ever constructed. It’s possible to ascend the dome for panoramic views of Florence.

Ponte Vecchio : Renowned for its shops and unique exterior, Ponte Vecchio is the oldest bridge in Florence. It’s also the only bridge in the city that wasn’t destroyed by Hitler in WWII. Originally, the shops on the bridge were occupied by butchers, farmers and tanners, but today they are mostly inhabited by jewellers and art dealers.

Piazzale Michelangelo : Large square south of the River Arno offering astonishing views over Florence. It’s especially brilliant at sunset however, it gets very busy so arrive early! This is also one of the best locations to photograph the cathedral.

Palazzo Vecchio : ‘Old Palace’ – built in the year 1299, this medieval fortified palace is the tallest building in Florence and it houses the town hall, roman ruins and various pieces of Renaissance art, including a copy of Michelangelo’s David sculpture.

Basilica of Santa Croce : Located near the Duomo, this Neo-Gothic basilica is the largest Franciscan church built in the middle ages. It’s also the burial site to more than 15,000 people, including many esteemed Italians like Michelangelo and Galileo.

Pisa : Not far from Florence lies the city of Pisa, best known for its illustrious 80m Leaning Tower. Located in the Piazza dei Miracoli (Square of Miracles), the 12the century bell tower is made up of 207 columns organised over 8 stories. The tower started leaning during construction due to the soft ground beneath it and, up until 1990, the tower leaned at angle of 10 degrees (now it leans at 4)! Also located in the elegant Piazza is the medieval white Cattedrale di Pisa, the domed San Giovanni Baptistery and the Camposanto Monumentale (‘Monumental Cemetery’).

Rome (Roma)

Italy’s capital city, Rome (the ‘Eternal City’), was founded in 753 BC and is world famous for being the home of the Romans. The Roman Empire spread from the city, across the Mediterranean, most of Europe and parts of northern Africa too. With grand palaces, ancient monuments, 280 fountains and more than 900 churches, modern Rome is a city of arts, history and food, with something interesting to see around every corner!

Travel tips

Rome can get extremely busy in the summer months so visiting in spring or autumn is probably best.

Purchase the Roma pass! It’s especially useful for skipping the line at the Colosseum. There are 2 different passes available…
72 hours = 2 free museums/archaeological sites/experiences + free public transport + lots of discounts = €52
48 hours = 1 free museum/archaeological site/experience + free public transport + lots of discounts = €32
Visit the website here for more info.

Things to see

Fontana di Trevi : The Trevi Fountain is located in the Rome’s city centre, on the side of Palazzo Poli. It’s said that throwing a coin into the fountain means you will return to Rome… roughly €3,000 is thrown into the fountain every day – that’s a million pounds a year! The money is collected periodically and donated to charity.

Colosseum : The great Rome Colosseum is the largest amphitheatre that the Romans built. It was once a gladiator arena that would seat more than 50,000 people and, according to the history books, 500,000 people and 1 million wild animals were killed. The last documented gladiator fight took place in 435 AD.

Roman Forum : This collection of Roman ruins, neighbouring the Colosseum, is said to have been in the centre of ancient Rome. Its buildings were used by the Romans for important political and religious activities.

Vatican City : Located within Rome, the Vatican City is actually an independent city-state and the smallest country in the world! It’s the seat of the Catholic Church and home to the Pope.
Click here to visit the Vatican City travel page.

Spanish Steps : Famous set of stairs from Piazza di Spagna to the Trinità dei Monti church. The unique and elegant design is popular with tourists.

Piazza Navona : …is one of Rome’s largest squares with three wonderful sculpted fountains and a baroque church. The square is commonly used by painters and, with several restaurants, it’s also a great place for food and live music.

Villa Borghese : …is a landscape garden/park in central Rome. Combining nature with Roman art, the park is dotted with sculptures, monuments and fountains, which have been created by famous artists over the years.

Piazza del Popolo : Another of Rome’s grand squares, situated next to Villa Borghese. The square has a tall Egyptian Obelisk in the middle and two identical-looking basilicas. Climb the steps for the best view.

Pantheon : Completed in 125 AD, this building is an ancient Roman temple dedicated to all gods of pagan Rome. The Pantheon is one of the best preserved and most influential buildings of ancient Rome.

Piazza Venezia : …is a sizable square in the city centre. It contains an impressive national monument dedicated to the first king of unified Italy and several other attractions.

Castel Sant’Angelo : …is a well-preserved 2nd century fortress/castle nearby to the Vatican City. In front of the castle sits the remarkable St. Angelo Bridge which is lined with statues of angels and saints.

Parco degli Acquedotti : Big park on the outskirts of Rome with the remains of two Roman aqueducts. These would’ve supplied water to toilets, baths, fountains and sewage systems in ancient Rome. Visit at sunset for the best experience.

Venice (Venezia)

The city of Venice is world famous for being one of the most unique cities on the planet. It’s known as the ‘Floating City’ and extends over a maze of 118 islands, 417 bridges and many canals/waterways, all in the middle of the Venetian Lagoon. Located in Italy’s northern Veneto region, the city was an independent republic for over 1000 years until Napoleon conquered it in the later 1700s. Overall Venice is a marvelous place to see however, it’s actually sinking at a rate of 1-2 mm per year and due to unsustainable amounts of tourism the local population has halved in the last 30 years!

Travel tips

Stay on the mainland for less expensive accomodation – it’s a 10 minute train journey away.

Exploring the intertwining streets of Venice is great fun, but be respectful… many of the locals are not fans of tourists!

Things to see

Grand Canal : …is an S-shaped channel running through Venice. It’s the largest and also the main canal / waterway in the city, so it’s very popular with boats and gondolas.

Rialto Bridge : …is the most famous bridge in Venice and the oldest of the four Grand Canal crossings. For many years it was the only way to cross the Grand Canal by foot.

St Mark’s Basilica : This imposing cathedral in St Mark’s square has five huge domes on its roof, a grand exterior and over 8,000 m² of mosaics inside (which took centuries to complete). If you want to go inside the cathedral then it’s best to purchase a ticket ahead of time, to avoid a long wait in a queue.

Palazzo Ducale : ‘Doge’s Palace’ is a fantastic 14th century Venetian Gothic style building on the edge of St Mark’s Square. Originally, the palace was the residence of the Doge (Venice’s ruler) and various Venetian political institutions. Today, Palazzo Ducale houses a museum with historical exhibitions.

St Mark’s Campanile : The bell tower of St Mark’s Basilica dominates Piazza San Marco and is a top attraction in Venice. It’s also the tallest building in the city so the views from the top are unmatched! Originally built between the 10th and 14th centuries, the tower fell down in 1902 and had to be reconstructed to the same design.

Piazza San Marco : Landmark square in the heart of Venice. It holds the basilica, campanile (bell tower), Doge’s Palace and a clock tower. Piazza San Marco has three symmetrical sides, with buildings containing various museums. There is also a promenade connecting it to the sea.

Bridge of Sighs : …is an enclosed bridge connecting the interrogation rooms in Doge’s Palace to Prigioni Nuove (‘New Prison’) on the opposing side of the canal. The Bridge of Sighs is a symbol of Venice and popular among visitors.

Church of San Giorgio Maggiore : Benedictine church on a small Venetian island of the same name. It can be accessed by boat from Piazza San Marco and you can climb the bell tower to look over the area. Napoleon used the island as a military area when Venice was under French rule.

Basilica di Santa Maria della Salute : …is a fascinating domed basilica at one end of the Grand Canal. It’s best seen from the opposite side of the canal or from the top of St Mark’s bell tower.

Burano : …is a peaceful island in the Venetian lagoon, away from the hubbub of Venice’s main tourist areas. Burano is famed for its colourful canalside buildings. Access the island using route 12 of Venice’s water bus service, which departs every 20-30 mins from Fondamente Nove (journey time = 40-45 mins).
